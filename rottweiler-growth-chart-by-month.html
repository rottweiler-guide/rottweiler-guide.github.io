A Rottweiler puppy grows rapidly, and understanding the Rottweiler growth chart by month is essential for monitoring health, preventing joint stress, and ensuring proper nutrition. This guide provides data-driven weight ranges, milestone expectations, and evidence-based feeding advice to help you track your Rottweiler’s development from 8 weeks to 24 months.

All weight and growth recommendations are based on veterinary guidelines from VCA Animal Hospitals and peer-reviewed canine growth literature indexed on PubMed. Always consult your veterinarian for individual health concerns.

Why tracking a Rottweiler growth chart by month matters for health

Rottweilers are large-breed dogs prone to hip dysplasia, osteochondritis dissecans (OCD), and obesity. A systematic growth chart helps identify abnormal weight gain or stunting early, allowing for diet adjustments before joint problems develop. The American Kennel Club (AKC) recommends monthly weight checks for large breeds during the first 18 months.

Overfeeding during the rapid growth phase (3–10 months) significantly increases the risk of developmental orthopedic diseases. A study published in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association found that Rottweilers fed a restricted diet had a 50% lower incidence of hip dysplasia than those fed freely. Using a Rottweiler growth chart by month helps owners fine-tune calorie intake. Learn more about our breed health resources.

Sex-based growth differences in Rottweilers

Male Rottweilers generally weigh 10–15% more than females at the same age. By 6 months, a male may reach 65 lbs while a female averages 55 lbs. The growth chart accounts for these differences, so always use sex-specific percentiles when tracking your puppy.

Most Rottweilers reach 90% of their adult height by 9–10 months, but continued muscle fill occurs until 20–24 months. The chart below shows typical weight ranges for both sexes.

Feeding guidelines aligned with the Rottweiler growth chart by month

Puppies need a large-breed puppy formula with controlled calcium (1.0–1.5% dry matter) and phosphorus (0.8–1.2%) to slow growth rate and avoid skeletal abnormalities. Feed three meals daily from 2–4 months, then two meals from 4–12 months. After 12 months, transition to an adult large-breed maintenance diet.

Calorie requirements by month

At 3 months, a typical Rottweiler needs about 900–1,200 kcal/day. By 6 months, that rises to 1,500–1,800 kcal/day. Between 8 and 12 months, caloric needs peak at approximately 2,000–2,400 kcal/day for males and 1,600–2,000 for females. Adjust based on body condition score (BCS) rather than weight alone.

Using a Rottweiler growth chart by month weekly allows you to detect if your puppy is gaining too fast—more than 5% body weight per week after 4 months is a red flag. Slow down growth by reducing portion sizes by 10–15% if needed.

Supplements to support growth

Glucosamine and chondroitin may benefit joint health, but the evidence base is limited. More important is ensuring adequate omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) from fish oil at 50–75 mg/kg body weight daily. A 2022 systematic review found that EPA/DHA supplementation reduced markers of cartilage degradation in young, large-breed dogs. Avoid calcium supplements unless prescribed by a veterinarian, as excess calcium worsens hip dysplasia.

Exercise limitations during the growth phase

Rottweiler puppies should never be encouraged to run on hard surfaces or jump from heights until growth plates close (around 18–24 months). Structured exercise includes short leash walks (5 minutes per month of age, twice daily) and supervised play on soft terrain. Avoid forced running, stair climbing, and agility until skeletal maturity.

A study on canine exercise physiology by VCA Hospitals notes that over-exercising large-breed puppies increases the risk of growth plate fractures and osteoarthritis. Instead of long runs, focus on mental stimulation like puzzle toys and basic obedience training.

Health screenings during each growth stage

Veterinary checkups should occur every 3–4 weeks for vaccine boosters until 16 weeks, then every 3–6 months through 24 months. At 6 months, schedule hip and elbow radiographs (PennHIP evaluation) to screen for dysplasia. According to the Orthopedic Foundation for Animals, Rottweilers rank among the top 10 breeds for hip dysplasia, with 20–30% affected.

Bloodwork at 12 months should include thyroid function tests, as hypothyroidism is common in Rottweilers. Early detection allows for treatment before clinical signs like weight gain and lethargy appear. For more information, visit our about page for breed health overviews.

Signs of abnormal growth to watch for

  • Limping or reluctance to rise – may indicate panosteitis (growing pains) or OCD
  • Bowing of forelimbs – possible hypertrophic osteodystrophy (HOD)
  • Excessive weight gain >15% above chart range – obesity risk
  • Failure to double birth weight by 4 months – possible malnutrition or illness

If you notice any of these signs, contact your veterinarian immediately. Do not attempt to diagnose or treat growth disorders at home. Refer to our contact page to find a local veterinary specialist.

Coefficient of growth: comparing your Rottweiler to breed standards

The coefficient of growth (COG) is a ratio of weight at a given age to predicted adult weight. By 4 months, a Rottweiler should be approximately 35–40% of its adult weight. By 6 months, 55–65%, and by 12 months, 80–90%. Using a Rottweiler growth chart by month alongside COG calculations gives a more precise health picture.

To calculate COG: divide current weight by predicted adult weight (midpoint of chart range). For a 6-month male at 72 lbs with a predicted adult weight of 122 lbs, COG = 0.59, or 59%. This aligns with the expected 55–65% range. Significant deviation from COG norms warrants a veterinary evaluation.

Growth rates in early versus late months

The most rapid growth occurs between 2 and 5 months, when puppies can gain 2–4 lbs per week. After 8 months, weight gain slows to 1–2 lbs per week, focusing more on muscle development than skeletal growth. By 18 months, weekly gains drop below 0.5 lbs. Tracking these rates via a Rottweiler growth chart by month prevents obesity.

A 2020 publication in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine noted that large-breed dogs gaining more than 2.5% of their body weight per week between 3–6 months had a 40% higher risk of developing osteoarthritis later in life. Use the chart to maintain a steady, moderate trajectory.

Common growth pitfalls and how to avoid them

Overfeeding during the plateau phase

Between 12–18 months, the Rottweiler growth chart by month shows only modest weight gain. Many owners continue feeding puppy portions, leading to rapid fat accumulation. Transition to an adult diet at 12 months, reducing calories by 15–20% as growth slows.

Underestimating exercise needs for mental growth

While physical exercise must be limited, mental stimulation is critical. Use obedience classes, scent work, and puzzle toys to occupy your Rottweiler’s mind. Boredom can lead to destructive behaviors that mimic hyperactivity, prompting owners to over-exercise. Our blog offers training exercises tailored to growth stages.

Ignoring breed-specific dental development

Rottweilers have a strong bite force, and their baby teeth (deciduous) are replaced by 6–7 months. Ensure appropriate chew toys to prevent retained baby teeth, which can cause misalignment and pain. Check your puppy’s mouth weekly and consult a vet if baby teeth remain beyond 8 months.

Environmental stressors that slow growth

Stress from loud noises, travel, or inconsistent routines can suppress appetite in sensitive Rottweilers. Provide a quiet feeding area and maintain a consistent daily schedule. If your puppy shows signs of stress anorexia for more than 48 hours, contact your veterinarian immediately.

Frequently asked questions about Rottweiler growth

At what age do Rottweilers stop growing?

Most Rottweilers reach their adult height by 12–14 months, but they continue filling out in muscle mass until 20–24 months. The growth chart by month accounts for both height and weight changes through 24 months.

How much should a 3-month-old Rottweiler weigh?

A 3-month-old male typically weighs 30–40 lbs, and a female 25–35 lbs. Use the chart above to compare your puppy’s weight monthly.

Are Rottweilers prone to hip dysplasia?

Yes, about 20–30% of Rottweilers develop hip dysplasia, according to OFA data. Controlled growth with a monthly chart and proper joint supplements can lower risk.

Is it safe to neuter my Rottweiler before 12 months?

Research suggests delayed neutering after 12 months—or after growth plate closure—reduces the risk of orthopedic injury and certain cancers. Discuss timing with your veterinarian.

Can I free-feed my Rottweiler puppy?

No. Free feeding leads to rapid, excessive weight gain. Scheduled meals (3 daily until 4 months, then 2 daily) based on a growth chart are essential for joint health.

What is the ideal body condition score for a growing Rottweiler?

Aim for a BCS of 4–5 out of 9. Ribs should be easily felt but not seen. Overly prominent ribs indicate underfeeding; no palpable ribs indicate obesity.

How often should I check my Rottweiler's weight?

Every 2–4 weeks during the first year. Record weight on the same scale at the same time of day. Use our Rottweiler growth chart by month to log results.

Do female Rottweilers mature faster than males?

Yes. Females typically reach adult height by 10–12 months, while males continue growing into their second year. The growth chart separates sexes for accuracy.

Can overfeeding cause hip dysplasia?

Overfeeding contributes to obesity and accelerated growth, which stresses developing joints. A controlled diet based on a monthly chart significantly reduces dysplasia risk.

When should I switch my Rottweiler to adult dog food?

Transition to adult large-breed maintenance diet at 12 months. The growth chart will show when weight gain stabilizes, confirming the appropriate time.

How much exercise is safe for a 6-month-old Rottweiler?

Two 15–20 minute walks per day plus supervised play. Avoid jogging, jumping, and hard surfaces until 18 months. Follow VCA guidelines cited above.

What should I do if my Rottweiler is below the growth chart range?

Consult your veterinarian to rule out parasites, malabsorption, or endocrine disorders. Do not increase feeding without guidance.

Can I use a human growth chart for my dog?

No. Canine growth curves differ significantly from human patterns. Always use a breed-specific chart like the one on this page.

When do Rottweiler growth plates close?

Growth plates close between 12–24 months for large breeds. Radiographs can determine closure precisely. Avoid high-impact activities until confirmed.

Is there a link between growth rate and cancer in Rottweilers?

Rapid growth may increase the risk of certain cancers, though evidence is mixed. Maintaining a slow, steady growth trajectory using a monthly chart is prudent. See our privacy policy for data sources.
